Prepare Your Practice for COVID-19

The WSMA, Washington state officials, and the state's health care community have mobilized in response to an outbreak of the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2 and its associated respiratory illness, COVID-19. Read WSMA's statement on the outbreak issued March 6, 2020. 

The WSMA is working to make sure you are equipped to handle your patients’ and community needs as the virus spreads. We urge you not to wait, but to prepare your practice now. Review the information below, updated as new details are available. For those of you working in an outpatient practice, please familiarize yourself and your staff with the Washington State Department of Health’s guidance for outpatient practices.

This page was updated March 18, 2020, and will be updated as new information is available.

Adopting Telehealth as COVID-19 Response

Larger practices and systems currently using telehealth technology are working to scale up those services as demand for COVID-19 testing increases. Smaller and midsized physician practices may want to consider adopting telehealth, which is recommended by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention to help mitigate the impact and prevent the spread of COVID-19. Telehealth can:

  • Enable people to access care and initial screenings from their home.
  • Minimize the demands on in-person primary care offices, urgent care centers, and emergency departments.
  • Minimize the use of personal protective equipment like gloves and masks.
  • Reduce the risk of exposure to patients and medical care teams.
  • Ensure that treatment is available for high-need patients.

DEA relaxes rules for prescribing controlled substances using telehealth

Under the Secretary of Health and Human Services public health emergency declaration, DEA-registered practitioners may now issue prescriptions for controlled substances to patients for whom they have not conducted an in-person medical evaluation, provided all of the following conditions are met:

  • The prescription is issued for a legitimate medical purpose by a practitioner acting in the usual course of his/her professional practice
  • The telemedicine communication is conducted using an audio-visual, real-time, two-way interactive communication system.
  • The practitioner is acting in accordance with applicable Federal and State law.

Read the DEA announcement. This exception will be effective while the public emergency declaration remains in effect.

HHS relaxes telehealth HIPAA rules, allows for video chat

The Health and Human Services (HHS) Office for Civil Right (OCR) has announced that it is relaxing HIPAA rules for physicians and other health care providers using telehealth in response to COVID-19. This change is effective immediately. Learn more.

Telehealth benefits for Medicare beneficiaries expanded

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services has expanded telehealth coverage for Medicare beneficiaries and will temporarily pay clinicians to provide telehealth services beginning March 6. As COVID-19 poses greater risks to seniors, this will allow Medicare beneficiaries to manage health issues and receive a wider range of services from the comfort and safety of their homes. Learn more.

Other telehealth updates

On March 13, 2020, the WSMA wrote to Gov. Inslee urging more flexibility on telehealth so that physicians are able to continue caring for their chronically ill patients. Read the letter in full here.

The Washington State Legislature has approved a bill requiring payment parity for telemedicine. Unfortunately, a key provision of the bill requiring payment parity for commercial plans doesn’t affect plans issued on or after Jan. 1, 2021. However, an emergency clause applies to other portions of the bill making them effective immediately. Medicaid contracts executed after the bill is signed into law will require payment parity for telemedicine services. Additionally, a provision that an in-person visit is required before store and forward is reimbursed is eliminated after the bill is signed into law.

Congress has approved and President Trump has signed a bill appropriating $8.3 billion in emergency funding to combat COVID-19. The bill allocates $500 million to expand telemedicine services for Medicare plans. Additionally, the Trump administration announced that a number of major insurance carriers (Blue Cross Blue Shield, Anthem, Humana and UnitedHealthcare) have agreed to provide coverage for telemedicine services, as well as waiving copays and extending coverage, for COVID-19 patients.

America’s Health Insurance Plans has compiled this list of individual insurance carriers’ response to the COVID-19 outbreak. While not all carriers operate in Washington state, the list details which are now offering telemedicine reimbursement and waivers for COVID-19-related treatment.

Medicare has created this website to answer COVID-19-related questions.

The Washington State Department of Labor and Industries (L&I) has issued a temporary telehealth policy. This policy will allow physicians and health care providers to treat injured workers from their homes with the goal of supporting the containment of COVID-19. While the policy will reimburse for telemedicine services, it will not provide or reimburse the patient for equipment necessary to receive the telemedicine services. If a worker does not have access to the necessary technology, a provider must work with the patient to find an alternative. This change is effective now through July 3, 2020 unless an extension is necessary.

OIC waives copays, deductibles, and preauthorizations for COVID-19 testing

Insurance Commissioner Mike Kreidler announced an emergency order to Washington state health insurers requiring them to waive copays and deductibles for any consumer requiring testing for COVID-19. The EO also requires that insurers regulated by the OIC must: allow a one-time early refill for prescription drugs; suspend prior authorization requirements for treatment or testing of COVID-19; and, if an insurer does not have enough medical providers in its network to provide testing and treatment for COVID-19, it must allow enrollees to be treated by another provider within a reasonable distance at no additional cost. The order is effective immediately and applies to all state-regulated health insurance plans and short-term limited duration medical plans until May 4, 2020. Read the OIC emergency order.
